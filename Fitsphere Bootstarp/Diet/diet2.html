Should You Try Carb Cycling? Decoding the Facts

Shweta Kumari
April 23, 2024

Carbohydrates, along with protein and fat, are vital macronutrients that give the body energy for overall health. While too many refined carbs can cause weight gain and health issues, the problem is not the carbs themselves but how much and what kind you eat. That’s why carb cycling, a new dietary approach, involves alternating between high and low-carb intake days instead of completely eliminating carbs.

What is Carb Cycling?

Carb cycling is a dietary approach that alternates between higher and lower carbohydrate intake days. The basic principle is to tailor carb consumption to meet changing needs over weeks, months, or a year. Initially favoured by bodybuilders and athletes, carb cycling is now gaining popularity among those interested in the ketogenic diet.

Aligning with one’s training schedule, carb cycling involves consuming more carbs on intense workout days and fewer on less demanding days. This strategy ensures the body receives necessary carbohydrates on high-carb days to fuel workouts and replenish glycogen stores. On low-carb days, the body depends on stored fat for energy. As a result, carb cycling creates cycles of high, medium, and low-carb days throughout the week.

People practising carb cycling also engage in calorie cycling as well. It involves consuming fewer calories on “low-carb days” and more calories on “high-carb days.” Additionally, some people choose to cycle their carbs within a single day. For example, one can concentrate on eating high-carb foods around their workout time while opting for low-carb foods during the rest of the day.

Summary

Carb cycling, alternating between higher and lower carbohydrate intake days, tailors consumption to changing needs over weeks. This strategy ensures the body receives necessary carbohydrates for fuel and glycogen replenishment on high-carb days, relying on stored fat for energy on low-carb days. People also engage in calorie cycling, adjusting intake based on carb days, with some opting for intra-day carb cycling, focusing high-carb foods around workouts.


Benefits of Carb Cycling

Cycling between low-carb days and high-carb days feels less restrictive and easier than maintaining a consistently low-carb diet. This approach also provides additional benefits, including:

1. Promote Metabolism

The core principle of carb cycling is to optimise carbohydrates for energy and recovery on active days while promoting fat utilisation on less active days. This strategy enhances metabolism by keeping it active and adaptable. High-carb days stimulate metabolism through the thermic effect of food, while low-carb days prompt fat metabolism, facilitating weight loss and efficient metabolic function.

2. Promotes Insulin Sensitivity

High-carb eating patterns often lead to spikes in blood sugar. Eating smaller, regular portions of carbs throughout the day supplies the body with less sugar at each meal, facilitating insulin’s function and enhancing insulin sensitivity. Furthermore, low-carb days prompt the body’s ability to utilise stored fats by improving insulin sensitivity.

3. Supports Weight Loss

Carb cycling achieves a balanced insulin release, levelling blood glucose and controlling cravings. This balance contributes to reduced caloric intake, supporting weight loss and overcoming plateaus.

Strategic limitation of carb intake on specific days further regulates overall carb and calorie consumption, which is crucial for effective weight management. As carb cycling is not recommended for long-term weight management, individuals should consider it only after exploring more sustainable nutritional strategies.

4. Improve Athletic Performance

Carb cycling is widely adopted by bodybuilders and athletes, especially during the cutting diet phase of physique competitions. By strategically incorporating low or no-carb days, competitors exploit the water-weight-reducing effect of depleting glycogen, altering muscle appearance on stage. On the other hand, increasing carbohydrate intake creates an energy surplus and promotes muscle gain.

Summary

Cycling between low-carb and high-carb days feels less restrictive and more sustainable than maintaining a consistently low-carb diet. This approach offers additional benefits, promoting metabolism by optimising carbohydrates for energy and recovery on active days while encouraging fat utilisation on less active days. It enhances insulin sensitivity, controls blood sugar spikes, and supports weight loss by achieving a balanced insulin release. Athletes use carb cycling strategically to improve performance, exploiting glycogen depletion for a leaner appearance on stage and optimising muscle gain.


Who Should Try Carb Cycling?

If you’re into endurance sports or follow a low-carb diet, carb cycling might work well for you. For endurance athletes like runners and cyclists, adjusting carbs, especially reducing them during intense training, can boost muscle glycogen. There is a rise in performance when carbs are reintroduced. Some find a lower-carb diet helpful for weight maintenance and overall health.

People with underlying metabolic issues or digestive issues might experience bloating and fatigue after consuming large amounts of carbohydrates. Therefore, this group can effectively benefit from carb cycling. Concentrating the majority of their carbohydrate intake around workouts could be advantageous for them.

Many who follow keto-like, high-fat, low-carb diets commonly use carb cycling. However, a high-fat diet is not a requirement to reap its benefits. You can successfully cycle your carbs with any kind of diet.

Summary

For endurance athletes or those on a low-carb diet, carb cycling suits well. It can help with enhancing muscle glycogen and performance upon carb reintroduction. Some find a lower-carb diet aids in weight maintenance and overall health. People with metabolic or digestive issues can effectively benefit from carb cycling by concentrating carb intake around workouts. While popular among keto-like, high-fat, low-carb dieters, carb cycling is not exclusive to high-fat diets.


How to Start Carb Cycling?

To start carb cycling, track your macros through an app or food journal. Determining daily carb grams is highly individualised, considering factors like body weight, age, sex, workout intensity, and listening to your body’s hunger cues. You must emphasise personalised approaches for optimal results.

On high-carb days, about 60% of calories come from complex carbs, while on low-carb days, some carbs are swapped for healthy fats. Alternatively, use 50 grams of carbs per day as a starting point for low-carb days, considering it a baseline for reaching ketosis. Customise the plan according to individual preferences and how your body responds.

If you are considering a carb cycle, a weekly plan could look like this:

  • Day 1: 50 grams carbs (Least intense training day)

  • Day 2: 100 grams carbs

  • Day 3: 150 grams carbs

  • Day 4: 200 grams carbs (High intense training day)

  • Day 5: 125 grams carbs

After day 5, you can cycle back to day one. Maintain consistent protein levels while modifying fat in alignment with carb adjustments. If carb intake rises, reduce fat consumption to balance the calorie shift. This approach better suits those aiming to achieve weight or body fat loss.

Summary

High-carb days should consist of around 60% calories from complex carbs, while on low-carb days, swap some carbs for healthy fats. Alternatively, start with 50 grams of carbs as a baseline for ketosis on low-carb days. Customise the plan based on personal preferences and body responses. A sample weekly plan might involve gradually increasing carbs from 50 to 200 grams, aligning with workout intensity. Also, maintain consistent protein intake while adjusting fat to balance calories for weight or body fat loss.

The Final Word

Carb cycling offers a flexible approach to managing carbohydrate intake, providing potential benefits for weight loss, muscle gain, and overall metabolic function. By alternating between high and low-carb days, individuals can tailor their nutrition to meet changing needs. However, it is essential to approach carb cycling cautiously, considering present health conditions and long-term sustainability.

The effectiveness of this approach relies on personalised planning, tracking, and consistency. While carb cycling may suit some individuals, it is not recommended as a long-term solution. Therefore, always consult with healthcare professionals or nutritionists before making significant changes to your diet.
