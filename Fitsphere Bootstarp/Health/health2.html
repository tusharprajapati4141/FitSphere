Orthorexia: Signs, Symptoms, and Solutions

Nahida
April 16, 2024

Knowing how different foods affect your body can be good for your health. But when this interest becomes an obsession with only eating “healthy” or “clean” foods and feeling intense fear about eating anything labelled “junk” or “bad,” it is called orthorexia nervosa. Orthorexia is a pathological fixation on eating only “pure” or “clean” foods. So, with this condition, you might eat three meals a day, but you are too focused on healthy food and miss out on essential nutrients.

The rise of social media influencers and trendy diets frequently spotlighting “superfoods” and demonising entire food categories like fats or carbs fuel this obsession. Those with orthorexia may receive accolades for their seemingly disciplined health choices, yet behind the scenes, they face malnutrition risks inadvertently induced by their restrictive eating patterns.


Orthorexia Meaning: Decoding The Disorder

Orthorexia nervosa is an intense preoccupation with healthy eating to the extent that it begins to disrupt daily life. Individuals with this disorder might limit their diet to a narrow range of foods deemed ‘pure’, like organic vegetables, excluding other vital food groups. This rigorous approach disrupts social meals and prevents the body from getting comprehensive nutrition, potentially leading to serious health repercussions.

Though not officially classified in the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual for Mental Disorders (DSM), orthorexia shares traits with recognised eating disorders. It can be a gateway to conditions like anorexia nervosa. Like other eating disorders, orthorexia nervosa is not a choice but develops from an unhealthy relationship with food. Many people feel pressured to fit into societal body image and health status, leading to strict diets and obsessions with healthy eating.

Summary

Orthorexia nervosa is the extreme preoccupation with consuming only ‘pure’ or ‘clean’ foods, often leading to the exclusion of essential nutrients, negatively impacting overall health. Not officially recognised as an eating disorder in the DSM, it still presents significant risks, potentially evolving into more severe eating disorders if not addressed.


Orthorexia vs. Anorexia

While orthorexia nervosa and anorexia nervosa both stem from the Greek word “rexia,” meaning “hunger,” their paths diverge drastically. “Ana” signifies “without,” reflecting anorexia’s hallmark of avoiding food intake, whereas “ortho” translates to “right,” portraying orthorexia’s pursuit of consuming what is deemed ‘correct’ or ‘pure’ food.

Summary

Orthorexia and anorexia, both characterised by the term “rexia,” hunger, display divergent motivations and manifestations. Anorexia focuses on weight control through food avoidance, while orthorexia obsesses over the healthfulness of food. Despite their differences, both disorders can lead to severe health and social consequences, underlining the importance of recognising and addressing these conditions.


Stages of Orthorexia Development

Orthorexia typically unfolds in two distinct stages:

Belief Formation

Initially, individuals adopt a belief system valuing certain dietary practices as superior, often influenced by trends like clean eating, paleo, or vegan diets. While aiming for health, these rigid food philosophies can inadvertently lead to the exclusion of essential nutrients.

Obsessive Behaviour

The second stage is marked by an intense preoccupation with food choices, meal planning, and food preparation, striving for an ideal of purity and perfection in the diet. This obsession can result in self-imposed food rules, with deviations leading to feelings of guilt and subsequent detoxes or cleanses, which pose their own health risks.

Understanding these stages helps in recognising the progression of orthorexia and the importance of addressing its underlying issues before they escalate.

Summary

Orthorexia evolves in two stages: the adoption of rigid dietary beliefs and the escalation into obsessive, rule-bound eating behaviour. Recognising these patterns is crucial for seeking timely intervention and fostering a healthier relationship with food.


Orthorexia Symptoms

While orthorexia nervosa lacks formal diagnostic criteria, its hallmark signs are hard to ignore. Central to this disorder is a stringent pursuit of dietary perfectionism, often leading to a compulsive need for control over food quality and purity. Additional signs and symptoms, according to the National Eating Disorders Association (NEDA), may include:

Obsession with Food Quality

Those with orthorexia fixate on consuming only what they consider ‘pure’ foods—organic, unprocessed, farm-fresh, and often raw or vegan. Their concern is less about quantity and more about the perceived cleanliness of their diet.

Cutting Out Entire Food Groups

People with orthorexia tend to be extremely strict about what they eat. They avoid anything they see as “bad” or “unhealthy.” For instance, they often cut out entire food groups, such as processed foods, sugar, meat, dairy products, carbohydrates, and gluten.

Emotional Turmoil When Breaking Dietary Rules

Deviating from their strict dietary rules triggers intense emotional responses such as anxiety, shame, and guilt. It highlights the deep psychological impact of the disorder.

Anxiety Around Specific Foods

Individuals with orthorexia often feel a strong urge to distance themselves from foods they consider forbidden. They might feel very uneasy or even leave a room if they see that food. A common behaviour pattern is skipping social gatherings to avoid encountering their “fear foods,” which can intensify the obsessive thoughts.

Weight Loss

An orthorexic diet is often unbalanced, leading to malnutrition. By believing that avoiding certain foods will improve health, people with orthorexia may unintentionally lower their nutrition levels by severely limiting food variety. This weight loss is typically not intentional.

As we delve deeper into understanding orthorexia, it becomes clear that recognising these symptoms is the first step toward addressing the disorder. Let us not take you through a self-assessment to help identify if you or someone you know may be experiencing these signs.

Summary

Orthorexia, characterised by an excessive focus on eating ‘pure’ foods, leads to significant nutritional and emotional consequences. Key indicators include an obsession with food quality, strict avoidance of certain food groups, emotional distress over dietary deviations, fear of specific foods, and unintentional weight loss. Recognising these symptoms is crucial for addressing the disorder and fostering healthier eating habits.

The Final Word

In the journey towards optimal health in a world saturated with social media’s filtered views on diet and fitness, the line between healthy eating and orthorexia can blur. The allure of ‘eating clean’ and following the perfect diet, often amplified by influencers and wellness gurus, can lead to a dangerous obsession with food purity. While orthorexia is not yet recognised officially in medical diagnostics, its impact is real and parallels other eating disorders, risking severe health and emotional well-being.
