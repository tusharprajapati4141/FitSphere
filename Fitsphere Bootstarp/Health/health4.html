Why Do I Feel Hungry All the Time?

Nahida
March 22, 2024

The sensation of hunger is a natural signal from your body indicating the need for food. While most people can comfortably go several hours between meals without feeling hungry, exceptions exist. At times, hunger may strike even when there is no actual need for food or calories. In such instances, factors such as an inadequate diet, specific lifestyle habits, or medications can contribute to an unchecked increase in appetite. However, leptin resistance often emerges as a key causative factor.

Leptin, a crucial hormone for appetite regulation, conveys feelings of satiety to the brain. However, resistance to its effects can disrupt this signal, causing hormonal imbalance and persistent feelings of hunger. The frequency of hunger also depends on the types of foods you consume, their impact on blood sugar, and the rate at which your body metabolises them.


The Role of Leptin and Ghrelin in Hunger

Leptin, often called the “satiety hormone” or the “starvation hormone,” is crucial in appetite and energy regulation. Produced by your body’s fat cells, leptin travels through the bloodstream to the hypothalamus, the brain’s control centre for eating patterns. High leptin levels signify ample fat stores, prompting the hypothalamus to suppress appetite. It creates a sense of fullness and diminishes the urge to eat.

Ghrelin, also known as the “hunger hormone” or Lenomorelin, is produced in the gut and primarily functions to stimulate appetite. It encourages increased food consumption, calorie intake, and fat storage. Higher ghrelin levels result in increased hunger, while lower levels promote a feeling of fullness.

As you consume food and accumulate more body fat, leptin levels rise, signalling a sense of satiety. Conversely, during periods of fasting or reduced fat stores, leptin levels decrease, and ghrelin levels rise, indicating the need to eat and replenish energy reserves. This counteraction between leptin and ghrelin helps the body manage appetite and maintain energy balance.

Summary

Leptin and ghrelin work together for appetite regulation and energy balance. Leptin, produced by fat cells, signals fullness and satisfaction as body fat increases, promoting reduced appetite. Alternatively, ghrelin stimulates hunger before meals, signalling low energy reserves and the need to eat. This interplay helps the body maintain a balance between hunger and satiety, ensuring proper energy regulation.


Leptin Resistance and Hunger

Persistent hunger could be a result of leptin resistance, which is a hormonal imbalance. Leptin resistance interferes with the communication between fat cells and the brain. Usually, high leptin levels indicate to the brain that sufficient energy is stored, signalling a natural reduction in the urge to eat. However, in leptin resistance, the brain becomes less responsive or fails to recognise this signalling process. As a result, despite high leptin levels in the body, the brain may not acknowledge them, leading to ongoing feelings of hunger.

When your brain fails to receive the leptin signal, it interprets it as if your body is in a state of starvation despite having ample energy stored. In response, your brain may alter your eating behaviour to regain body fat, leading to increased hunger or a reduction in energy levels to burn fewer calories at rest.

Summary

Leptin resistance, a hormonal imbalance, disrupts communication between fat cells and the brain, affecting hunger signals. High leptin levels typically indicate sufficient energy storage, reducing the urge to eat. However, in resistance, the brain becomes less responsive, causing ongoing hunger despite high leptin levels. This lack of signalling may prompt altered eating behaviours, with the brain interpreting the body as starved, potentially increasing hunger.


Causes of Leptin Resistance

Leptin resistance and obesity are interconnected. When you are overweight, the more body fat you have, the higher your leptin levels go (hyperleptinemia). But having a lot of leptin can make your body less responsive to its signals, and that’s called leptin resistance.

This lack of leptin sensitivity can lead to continued overeating, causing an accumulation of more body fat and contributing to obesity. While leptin resistance is a factor, it’s essential to understand that various factors, including genetics and lifestyle, also influence obesity.

Summary

Leptin resistance and obesity go hand-in-hand. When overweight, higher body fat results in increased leptin levels (hyperleptinemia), leading to leptin resistance. This insensitivity contributes to persistent overeating, accumulating more body fat and fostering obesity. Factors like inflammation, elevated free fatty acids, and high leptin levels contribute to leptin resistance by disrupting signalling mechanisms.


Lifestyle Changes to Manage Leptin Resistance

While medication can assist in managing leptin resistance, here are certain lifestyle changes that can be beneficial:

Improve Sleep Cycle

Lack of sufficient sleep, poor sleep habits, and disrupted circadian rhythms increase the risk of leptin resistance. Therefore, improving leptin sensitivity is possible by ensuring adequate sleep.

To ensure sufficient sleep, establish a consistent bedtime before 10 pm and aim for 7-9 hours of quality rest.

Regular Exercise

Consistent exercise plays a crucial role in reducing body fat, maintaining a healthy weight, and lowering leptin levels. By incorporating regular workouts into your routine, you enhance the body’s capacity to regulate appetite, decrease abdominal obesity, and optimise metabolic health.

Supplements

Chinese cinnamon (Cinnamomum cassia) and Gymnema sylvestre are herbs that have the potential to regulate appetite by impacting leptin, glucose, and insulin levels. Incorporating these herbs into your diet, under guidance, may contribute to better appetite control and overall metabolic health. Summary

While medication can be a valuable aid in managing leptin resistance, incorporating lifestyle changes is equally beneficial. Adopt an anti-inflammatory diet with fibre-rich foods, veggies, and lean proteins. Go gluten-free for better appetite control. Practice time-restricted eating and ensure 7-9 hours of quality sleep. Regular exercise helps manage weight and lower leptin levels.

The Final Word

Persistent hunger may arise from various factors, including hormonal imbalances like leptin resistance. Additionally, lifestyle-related factors such as inadequate sleep, poor nutrient balance, distracted eating, stress, and medication effects can also influence appetite.
